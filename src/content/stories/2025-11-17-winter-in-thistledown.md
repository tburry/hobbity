The hobbits returned home to Thistledown. Over the long winter months, something changed in each of them. They discovered they could wield magic—small tricks at first. Boffo could portion food with perfect precision. Wedge could conjure fireflies that danced around his hand for three blinks. Turnip could untangle any rope or string with a single pull. The visions visited their dreams—not every night, not always vividly, but they returned. Plague, flood, undeath. Perhaps too much cheese before bed. Perhaps something more.

## Turnip's Winter

That winter, Turnip avoided the woods entirely, except for short forays at dawn when he believed shadows would be at their weakest. He brought a walking stick on these excursions, though he never leaned on it, preferring to hold it like a cudgel. And he still kept the haft of the hatchet that he had shattered on the Hermit's forearm when it had grabbed at Boffo. More than once, he beat tree stumps just to see if they rang hollow. Sometimes they did. This confirmed nothing but made him feel justified.

His nights grew restless. He kept the lantern lit and worried about his stock of oil, but not enough to conserve it. He checked the windows three times before sleep. He left his slippers by the door pointing outward, in case "something tries to come in wearing them," a precaution he could not fully explain except to say it seemed sensible. He salted the threshold once a week. He salted it again after snowfall, because you never know what gets tracked in on the drifts.

He took to writing. He began cataloguing oddities around Thistledown that no one else thought odd: that the foxes were quieter, that the owls were flying wider circles, that the Hedgebrooks had a baby who never cried, that a scarecrow two fields over seemed to have changed hats despite no one touching it. He wrote these things down in a small notebook which he kept in his vest. The notebook grew thick with his observations and before long it had become a diary of sorts.

The thing that threw him off the most was that magic was real. That changed everything.

> I confess I have been thinking far too much, which Mum always said was dangerous for a hobbit, especially in winter when the cold gets into your bones and your nerves start hopping about like field mice, so I decided to put my thoughts down here, though I never meant to keep a diary because diaries feel like invitations to one's future self to look back and say, "What on earth were you doing, Turnip, you ninny," but the trouble is that ever since the business with the Hermit I keep remembering things at odd angles, as though the memories themselves are leaning, and if I don't pin them down somewhere they will keep rolling around in my head like apples escaping a basket on a sloped floor and getting bruised and then all you can do is bake them into pie, but then you can't separate one thing from another and just have to savour the lot of them as a whole, which is tasty but doesn't help the old cogitations.
>
> Anyway, the thing is I had another dream, and I know it was a dream because I woke up, but part of me wonders if that is the only difference, because it felt real in the way a memory does when you're suddenly reminded of it by a smell or a sound you didn't expect, and the details were sharper than dreams usually allow.
>
> I stood in front of the Hermit's home again—except writing "home" feels wrong, as if I'm giving the place too much credit. It was a hobbit-hole, like I saw in the woods, a tall hobbit-hole that truth be told had no business being so tall, because it was built into a hill but then the hill rose up too far and the round green door had a second row of windows perched above it like eyebrows drawn by someone who had only ever heard rumours of face shapes but had never actually seen a face. It reminded me of the Alewarden's burrow after he tried to expand upward without asking permission from the Thistledown Planning Committee, except his only sagged and this one… leaned forward, just a little, like it was interested in me, which was more unsettling than if it were disinterested, which although it's impolite is a more natural attitude for a hobbit hole, architecturally speaking.
>
> Now I know Wedge says he saw an enormous skull and Boffo says he saw a drafty old cabin, but that's not what I saw, and not what I would have wanted to see, if it comes to that, but then the world doesn't always show itself to us in the shape we'd rather see, and even when it does you'd better be doubly careful. Wedge told me the other day that if a house can choose how to appear, it can also choose what to hide, which is a thought that makes me want to crawl under the covers and never come out.
>
> Still, like Boffo says, "You can worry all morning, or you can have breakfast. Breakfast works better." I had three breakfasts this morning, and I have to say he's right. He also says, "Things look less scary after breakfast," which is probably true, though I've found it depends very much on what was in the breakfast and whether there's sufficient jam. Boffo always says things that are profound.
>
> Anyway, I was standing there in front of the too-tall hill, and he stepped out—the Hermit that is—with his beard blowing in the breeze (which there wasn't one), and his face stretched wrong like a sheet of pastry rolled too thin, and his eyes glowing with that hungry pumpkin-orange light. He asked if I'd brought pie. I had not. He looked disappointed. Deeply. I woke up with my mouth dry and my feet cold and ate half a loaf of seedcake without even slicing it, just chewing and trying not to think about how wide his jaw opened.

## Wedge's Winter

During a get-together at Wedge's messy little home sometime in the deep of winter, over a steaming cup of tea and the requisite number of cakes, Wedge ruminates upon the adventuring life with his colleagues.

> Only a few years ago, on a night quite like this, all cold and snowy, and best used for staying indoors and warming the belly and the heart, my older sister, Willow, began her labours with her first child. As it happened, I was visiting her and having a cup of tea, just like we're doing now, when the weather turned foul, and her husband, such as he is, was waylaid by the storm and drink. Our mother had gone out to fetch him, she having been nested next to Willow for some weeks in anticipation of this very moment, and was likewise laid by.
>
> That left just Willow and myself in her modest home when the show began, and without recounting the steps that brought me to the foot of the bed, and the dispensation of modesty required by both of us, I can tell you that I was...front and centre as it were...when she issued forth her eldest child, and it was I that not only supported the effort, but pulled young Odo into the world, amidst the wet and fleshy chaos of the moment. Everything turned out fine, as you know, minus a bit of scandal about my role as midwife to my own sister, but fortunately, her husband's truancy was of greater transgression, and my experience is now a footnote in the entire affair.
>
> I say all this, because I have been thinking about the Gomwick Fair last year, and the dispensation of modesty and abandonment of manners that were required to pull the whole thing off. And I think that is the very nature of adventure and adventuring; we put aside our manners and do a sticky job that others don't want to, or can't do. All to great reward. A risk and a return, just like we saw at the Spottle table. Just like we've seen in the very trappings at Toppohill; that is the finest house I've ever seen, and it's inhabited by the most impertinent hobbit I ever met, saving that villain, Chubb, who I've tried to forget these last seasons. But that master Niss is cheeky; sending us to feed a monster from our nightmares, without telling us we're meant to feed it, or that IT is a monster. He meant to leave those bits out, or we would never had the courage for the errand. But now we do, and on top of the coin and nightmares we all brought out of the situation, I think we've all been left with a bit of a...desire...for further impertinence. I at least do, and I mean to feed that desire, and find myself a home...

— and he pauses to look about his small, dark and messy rooms —

> ...like Toppohill.

Wedge spent the seasons reading any books he could find that were as close to forbidden lore and adventures as he could get, as well as practicing petty thefts, which he always undid by way of replacing the pilfered items after he'd had a moment to enjoy his clandestine victories.

## Boffo's Winter

Boffo returned in the crisp days after the festival, following the familiar path to the hobbit hole where his mama, grandmama, and great-grandmama waited. Of their fifty-three kin, he was the last still at home, and the three women greeted him with such warm joy you'd think he'd hauled summer back in his pack.

They fussed over his pie-eating ribbons and trophy until the shine wore thin. Boffo only smiled, loving them all the more for it. In his room, he set his awards in a corner and decided that would be their final place. Little hobbits soon gathered, wide-eyed, asking how he'd earned such treasures. Boffo only shrugged: "I love Miss Daisy's pies."

The leaves had fallen since they left, so Boffo raked his family's hobbit hole, then the neighbour's, then the next. By week's end, he had coaxed nearly the whole village's leaves into one tremendous pile on a fallow field. The littlest hobbits vanished into it each day, entering after breakfast and tumbling out at dusk.

At the first snowfall, Boffo decided he ought to learn how Miss Daisy made her pies. He tried his best to recreate them, but the results were never quite the same pie twice. A little dust, a few mystery specks, and plenty of guesswork slipped into his crusts. Yet, the pies were always edible and proudly homemade, and whenever Boffo served them, everyone somehow felt they had gotten just the right-sized piece.

When winter froze the stream and the Miller's Pudding Hound couldn't turn the winter mill-wheel anymore, Boffo stepped in without fuss. He simply knocked on the miller's door and said, "Pie's not pie without flour," before taking the hound's place at the wheel. Each morning he pushed it round, grinding flour for the whole village; each afternoon he baked pies. And so the village kept its flour through the cold months until spring thawed the stream and the Pudding Hound's pups were born.

But winter dragged on, and the food stores thinned. No hobbit was in danger of starving, heavens no! But only five meals a day makes for a gloomy season. It just so happened that on one of Boffo's nightly walks, for he liked the crunch the snow made under his feet and didn't mind the cold, he came upon a curious patch of nettles poking through the snow. They were the tallest, thickest, and stinging-iest, yet somehow the most succulent nettles a hobbit ever met. They stung something fierce to pick, but Boffo only grunted, "Pie's pie, and this'll do." So he stepped into the patch and saved the village's stomachs, and all the hobbits enjoyed their afternoon tea with nettle pie for the rest of the winter until spring finally stirred.

Snowdrops bloomed first, cheering every hobbit heart. Then came the crocuses, bright enough to wake a sleepy village and perfect for crocus-bulb scones. After that came fried fiddleheads with sugar, then blackthorn blossom tarts, then tulip-bud cake, and then at last savory allium sammiches—the true heralds of spring!

## Buford's Letter

In the early weeks of spring, each received a letter from [Buford](/hobbity/world/npcs/#buford-niss). Boffo squinted at his, tongue out in deep concentration, before handing it to his great-grandmama. She read it, then passed it to his grandmama, who passed it to his mama, who finally read it aloud so Boffo could hear what the letter had to say.

> To the Esteemed Master Lunderbunk,
>
> Greetings my friend! I hope your winter has been short and warm, and that the lengthening days of Spring bring you hope and prosperity to you and your family.
>
> I write to you for I am in need of your help. I have received an answer from "Our Friend" in the Old Woods, and have made plans to pursue the matter further. However, I have been laid low by time and illness and can scarcely rise from my bed these days. I am down to a scant three meals of porridge and fish a day, and my doctor has restricted me to only brandy and cider for drink. It is unbearable! When they think I am asleep I hear them tut-tuting and saying I may only have a few short months to live, but this is nonsense! I intend to still be here, playing Spottle and enjoying my home, for many years yet to come.
>
> But I need legs, you see. I am stuck in this bed, and questions that need answers are not to be found within my home. I need young legs to travel in my stead and ask those questions for me, then bring me back those answers.
>
> I have thought of you, and your two stalwart companions, as perfectly capable and well suitable for the task, so I implore you to accept my summons and come see me at Toppo Hill as soon as you are able to travel. We will discuss the particulars when you are here.
>
> And do not worry, you will be generously compensated for your time and efforts. In fact, you will find several coins that I have sent along with this letter to compensate you for your travelling expenses to bring you to Gomwick, as well as provide an example of more to come, if you agree to pursue this adventure in my stead.
>
> I Remain At Your Service,
>
> The Hon. Horace Buford Hockwallop Niss, Esq.
>
> Toppo Hill House Gomwick, Heartshire

Included with the letter were a gold coin and twenty silver pieces for travelling expenses.

As Boffo's mama read, Boffo squinted harder and harder; he squinted from one eye to the next. And when she finally read, "I Remain At Your Service, The Honourable Horace Buford Hockwallop Niss, Esquire," Boffo kept right on squinting, and he didn't stop for the rest of the day.

The next day Boffo was packed and ready. He hugged his mama, then his grandmama, then his great grandmama. "I'll come home again," he said with a nod. And off he went.
